most people are not honest with you. they are being nice, which is a different thing.

## the problem with nice

flattery is one of the quiet ways progress dies. someone shows you a thing they made, you tell them it's great because that is the polite move, and they walk away feeling good and knowing nothing. the work does not get better. multiply that across a company and you get a whole building of people gently lying to each other, everyone a little worse at their job than they could be.

the feedback that actually changed my work was never gentle. it was blunt. this part is confusing. this does not do what you said it does. why does this exist. it stung for about ten seconds and then it saved me weeks. now i trust the people who hand me the ugly version, and i trust the ones who only ever say "looks good" a little less each time.

so i try to give the real version back, even when it is uncomfortable. one thing matters here, because it is easy to get wrong. this is not the same as being cruel.

> cruelty is just noise with confidence.

it makes people defensive and teaches them nothing. what i mean is the specific, unflattering truth, said because i actually want the thing to be good. that is the whole difference. rude for sport helps no one. honest to the point of uncomfortable helps everyone, eventually.

## we need more builders

there is no shortage of smart people. there is a shortage of people who make things.

> smart is cheap now, you can practically rent it.

what is rare is someone who sits down and turns an idea into a thing that exists, works, and that other people can actually use. creation is upstream of almost everything good. a built thing becomes a product, a product becomes a company, a company becomes jobs, and jobs become people with a little more room to breathe. it sounds grand but it is mostly mechanical. somebody has to make the first thing.

i would rather be one of the people who makes the first thing than one of the hundred with perfect opinions about what everyone else should build.

## simple is the hard part

this one took me the longest to learn.

anyone can add. more features, more words, more cleverness. adding is easy, and it feels like progress. the real discipline is in what you leave out: the system that does one thing you can explain in a sentence, the paragraph with nothing left to cut. simple looks effortless, which is exactly why it is so hard. you only get there by throwing away every version that was trying too hard. funny how that works. the simplest things are usually the ones that took the most effort to reach.

so that is the whole thing, said as plainly as i can. build things. tell the truth about them. keep them simple. then do it again.
