## The problem

I built Screenshot Brain to search my screenshots by meaning: type "the error
about the expired token" and get the right image back, even if the word
"token" never appears on screen. The demo looked magic. The problem with magic
demos is you have no idea if they actually work — you type the three queries
you know succeed and call it done.

So the real question wasn't "can it search?" It was: **how good is the search,
and how would I even know?**

## What I tried

I built a small evaluation harness before adding a single feature:

- A labeled set of ~120 (query, correct-screenshot) pairs drawn from my own
  library, written before I looked at what the model returned.
- A metric that matches the task: recall@k and mean reciprocal rank, not raw
  cosine similarity, because what I care about is "is the right image in the
  top few?"
- A fixed test command so every change reports the same numbers on the same
  data.

The point was to make quality a number I could watch move, not a vibe.

## What broke

The first honest run was humbling: **recall@5 was 0.61.** Four out of ten
searches didn't surface the right screenshot in the top five.

Digging into the misses, most failures weren't the model — they were the
pipeline around it: OCR dropping text on low-contrast UI, and my chunking
splitting a single screenshot's text so no chunk held the whole thought.

## The number

After fixing OCR preprocessing and stopping the over-eager chunking,
**recall@5 went from 0.61 → 0.88** on the same held-out set. The embedding
model never changed. The eval was what told me where the real bug was.

## What I'd do differently

- Write the eval set *first*, next time from day zero — I lost a week trusting
  a demo.
- Expand the labeled set; 120 pairs is enough to catch big regressions, not to
  trust small deltas.
- Log every miss automatically so the failures come to me instead of me
  hunting for them.

The lesson that generalizes: a demo tells you the good case exists. An eval
tells you how often the bad case happens. Only one of those is worth trusting.
