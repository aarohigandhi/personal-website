most people are not honest with you. they are being nice, which is a different thing.

i've started to think flattery is one of the quiet ways progress dies. someone shows you a thing they made, and you tell them it's great — because that's the polite move, and you'd rather not be the one who makes it awkward. they walk away feeling good and knowing nothing. the work doesn't get better. multiply that by every conversation in a company and you get a whole building of people gently lying to each other, all of them a little worse at their jobs than they could be.

the feedback that actually changed my work was never gentle. it was closer to blunt — "this part is confusing," "this doesn't do what you said it does," "why does this exist." it stung for about ten seconds and then it saved me weeks. i've learned to trust the people who will hand me the ugly version. i trust the ones who only ever say "looks good" a little less every time.

so i try to give the real version back, even when it's uncomfortable. one thing i want to be clear about, because it's easy to get wrong: this is not the same as being rude. cruelty is just noise with confidence — it makes people defensive and teaches them nothing. what i mean is the specific, unflattering truth, said *because* i actually want the thing to be good. that's the whole difference. rude-for-sport helps no one. honest-to-the-point-of-uncomfortable helps everyone, eventually.

## we need more builders

there is no shortage of smart people. there is a shortage of people who make things.

smart is cheap now — you can practically rent it. what's rare is someone who sits down and turns an idea into a thing that exists, works, and that other people can actually use. that act — creation — is upstream of almost everything good. a built thing becomes a product; a product becomes a company; a company becomes jobs; jobs become people with a little more room to breathe. it sounds grand but it's mostly mechanical. somebody has to make the first thing.

i'd rather be one of the people who makes the first thing than one of the hundred people with perfect opinions about what everyone else should build.

## simple is the hard part

this is the one that took me longest to learn.

anyone can add. more features, more words, more cleverness — adding is easy and it *feels* like progress. the actual discipline is in what you leave out: the system that does one thing you can explain in a sentence, the paragraph with nothing left to cut. simple looks effortless, which is exactly why it's so hard — you only get there by throwing away every version that was trying too hard. funny how it works: the simplest things are usually the ones that took the most work to arrive at.

so that's the whole thing, said as plainly as i can:

build things. tell the truth about them. keep them simple.

then do it again.
